Psychologists such as E. Durban and John Bowlby have argued that aggression is inherent in human nature. It is fueled by sublimation and projection, when a person turns his discontent into prejudice and hatred towards other races, religions, nations or ideologies. According to this theory, the state creates and maintains a certain order in local society and at the same time creates the basis for aggression in the form of war.
Scientists who study evolutionary psychology tend to argue that human wars are analogous to the behavior of animals fighting over territory or competing for food or a mate. Animals are aggressive by nature, and in the human environment such aggressiveness translates into wars. However, with the development of technology, human aggression has reached such a limit that it begins to threaten the survival of the entire species.
If war is an integral part of human nature, as many psychological theories suggest, it can never be completely eradicated.
Sigmund Freud regarded aggression as one of the basic instincts determining the psychological "springs," the orientation and meaning of human existence, and based on this position.
